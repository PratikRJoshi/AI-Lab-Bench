## Analysis

### Key Claim Validation

1. **"Ideal omega-6 to omega-3 ratio is 4–5:1; restaurant oil-heavy diets skew it to 15:1."**
   — **Supported.** The optimal ω-6:ω-3 ratio is debated but most guidelines recommend 4:1 to 10:1. The Western diet typically produces 15–17:1. Indian restaurant cooking reliant on refined vegetable oils (high ω-6) can similarly skew this ratio. ICMR guidelines support a ratio closer to 5–10:1.

2. **"Millet ground to maida-like fine powder loses its glycemic advantage."**
   — **Supported.** The glycemic benefit of whole grains comes from intact fiber, bran, and slowly digestible starch — all of which are disrupted by fine milling. Studies on finger millet and pearl millet confirm that fine-milled flour has a significantly higher glycemic response than coarsely ground or whole-grain forms (Shukla et al., 2009).

3. **"Real food delivers vitamins and minerals bundled with fiber and plant chemicals that supplements cannot replicate."**
   — **Supported.** Food matrix effects are well-documented: nutrients in whole foods are absorbed at different rates due to fiber, co-occurring phytochemicals, fat content, and physical structure — collectively superior to isolated pill equivalents. This is the foundational argument for "food first" nutrition.

4. **"Coffee reduces non-heme iron absorption by ~40–70%."**
   — **Supported.** Multiple studies confirm that coffee (via chlorogenic acids) reduces non-heme iron absorption by 39–73% depending on dose and timing (Morck et al., 1983, *American Journal of Clinical Nutrition*). The video correctly specifies this applies to plant-source (non-heme) iron, not heme iron from red meat.

5. **"Soaking rajma/chana for several hours reduces phytic acid by up to 85%."**
   — **Supported.** Soaking and discarding soak water reduces phytic acid by 50–85% in legumes depending on duration, temperature, and pH. Fermentation and cooking further reduce it. Discard of soak water is appropriate advice.

6. **"Calcium carbide ripening is unsafe; ethylene gas (up to 100 ppm) is the food-safe alternative."**
   — **Supported.** Calcium carbide releases acetylene gas that mimics ethylene but may carry phosphine and arsenic impurities. It is banned in India under the Prevention of Food Adulteration Act. Exogenous ethylene at <100 ppm is approved by FSSAI and FDA as safe when properly applied.

7. **"Deep frying works via a steam jacket that keeps oil from penetrating until the food is lifted out."**
   — **Supported.** This is a well-established food science principle. Surface water flashes to steam at ~100°C, creating positive pressure that prevents oil ingress while the Maillard reaction occurs on the surface. Oil uptake primarily occurs on food removal when the steam pressure drops.

8. **"Millets were more common in pre-colonial India; the shift to rice/wheat was driven by British colonialism and the 1960s Green Revolution."**
   — **Supported.** Historical dietary records and archaeobotanical evidence confirm jowar, bajra, ragi and other millets as dietary staples across India until the 20th century. The Green Revolution preferentially developed and subsidized high-yield wheat and rice varieties, marginalizing millets. Colonial revenue policies restructured cropping patterns. Documented in food history scholarship.

9. **"A nine-year-old entering puberty is within the normal global average window (girls 8–13, boys 9–14)."**
   — **Supported.** The WHO and American Academy of Pediatrics define precocious puberty as before age 8 in girls and age 9 in boys. The historical Indian pattern of later puberty (~13–14) was partly due to undernourishment. Improved nutrition and childhood obesity are well-documented drivers of earlier puberty onset.

10. **"A short nap (15–30 minutes) doesn't cause weight gain; long naps (>1 hour) may signal underlying metabolic issues rather than cause them."**
    — **Supported.** Short naps improve alertness and have cardiovascular benefits (Naska et al., 2007, *Archives of Internal Medicine* — Mediterranean siesta study). Long daytime naps as an independent cause of weight gain is not supported; they are more likely a symptom of obesity and poor nighttime sleep.

11. **"Cooked rice: ~200 kcal/cup; measured originally via bomb calorimeter; now calculated from macronutrient fractions."**
    — **Supported.** The Atwater general factor system (carbs: 4 kcal/g, protein: 4 kcal/g, fat: 9 kcal/g) is the basis for food labeling worldwide, derived from original bomb calorimeter measurements.

12. **"Greek yogurt has 2–3× more protein than regular curd because whey is strained out."**
    — **Supported.** Straining removes whey (water + lactose), concentrating casein. Regular Indian curd: ~3–4 g protein/100g; Greek yogurt: ~8–10 g protein/100g. Commercial varieties may add skim milk powder or ultrafilter, further increasing protein.
