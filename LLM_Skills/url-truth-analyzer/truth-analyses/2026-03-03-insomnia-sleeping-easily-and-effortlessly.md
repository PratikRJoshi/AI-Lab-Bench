## Summary

This Waking Up course session presents Mindful CBT for insomnia, explaining the two-process model of sleep regulation (circadian and homeostatic drives), common behavioral and cognitive factors that worsen insomnia, and evidence-based techniques including sleep restriction therapy, sleep hygiene, and mindfulness. It recommends CBT as first-line treatment for chronic insomnia and advises against alcohol as a sleep aid.

## Analysis

### EBM SORT Analysis

#### Pass 1 — Four Analysis Lenses

**1. Safety lens**  
The content does not discuss medications, supplements, or invasive procedures. The recommended interventions (sleep restriction, stimulus control, sleep hygiene, mindfulness) are behavioral and carry minimal physical risk. The transcript correctly cautions that alcohol “can help you fall asleep, but it makes sleep worse overall” and advises avoiding it as a sleep aid. No absolute risks are quantified, but the absence of pharmacologic recommendations reduces safety concerns. No harms, side effects, or contraindications are omitted in a concerning way.

**2. Outcomes lens**  
The main claim—that CBT for insomnia is first-line treatment—is backed by patient-oriented outcomes (POEMs): sleep onset latency, wake after sleep onset, total sleep time, and sleep efficiency, which map to symptom relief and quality of life. The transcript references “simple changes that are known to relieve insomnia” and “sleep experts typically recommend CBT programs as the first line treatment for chronic insomnia.” The sleep restriction technique (limiting time in bed to actual sleep time plus ~30 minutes) is described in line with standard CBT-I protocols. Outcomes are patient-centered rather than surrogate markers.

**3. Risk of bias lens**  
The content does not cite specific studies, funding sources, or conflicts of interest. It presents consensus recommendations and standard CBT-I components. The two-process model (circadian and homeostatic sleep drives) is a well-established framework (Borbély model) with broad support. Sleep restriction therapy has been tested in multiple RCTs. The recommendation to avoid alcohol as a sleep aid aligns with systematic review evidence. No cherry-picking, undisclosed conflicts, or industry influence are evident. The main limitation is the lack of explicit citations in the audio itself.

**4. Total evidence lens**  
CBT-I is recommended as first-line treatment by major guidelines (e.g., ACP, AASM). Multiple meta-analyses of RCTs support its efficacy. Sleep restriction is a core component of CBT-I with its own meta-analytic support. The alcohol warning is consistent with a 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis showing that alcohol disrupts sleep architecture (including REM) even at low doses. The 65°F (18°C) sleep temperature recommendation is widely cited in sleep hygiene guidance, though individual variation exists and older adults may prefer slightly warmer temperatures.

#### Pass 2 — SORT Grade

**Grade A** — Consistent, good-quality patient-oriented evidence.

The central recommendation (CBT-I as first-line treatment for chronic insomnia) is supported by:
- Meta-analyses of RCTs showing clinically meaningful improvements in sleep onset latency, wake after sleep onset, and sleep efficiency
- Guideline endorsement (ACP, AASM)
- Sustained benefits without pharmacologic risks

The specific techniques (sleep restriction, sleep hygiene, stimulus control) are components of CBT-I with RCT and meta-analytic evidence. The two-process model is foundational sleep science. The alcohol warning is supported by systematic review evidence. The content aligns with current clinical practice and does not make unsupported or exaggerated claims.
