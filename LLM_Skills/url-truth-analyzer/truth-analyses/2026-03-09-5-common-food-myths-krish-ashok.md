# Truth Analysis: 5 Common Food Myths — Krish Ashok Reveals

**Source URL**: https://youtu.be/V-JqbllGaAs?si=VfjLSkbOOnU9hx-L  
**Analyzed**: 2026-03-09  
**Content type**: General Science (food science / science communication)

## Summary

An interview-style video with Krish Ashok (science communicator, author of "Masala Lab") debunking common Indian food myths: microwaves cause cancer, natural sugars (jaggery/honey) are healthier than white sugar, refrigerating food destroys nutrients (loss of "prana"), and the misuse of relative vs. absolute risk statistics to scare people about carcinogens.

## Analysis

### Claim Validation

**Claim 1: "Microwaves will cause cancer — that's a myth."**  
**Status: Supported**  
Microwave ovens use non-ionizing radiation (radio waves, ~2.45 GHz) to heat water molecules. Non-ionizing radiation does not have sufficient energy to break chemical bonds or damage DNA. There is no peer-reviewed evidence that microwave cooking increases cancer risk. This is a longstanding consensus position of the WHO, FDA, and cancer research organizations. The myth persists culturally but has no scientific basis.

**Claim 2: "Jaggery is up to 85% the same sucrose. Your body can't tell the difference between sucrose from jaggery and sucrose from white sugar."**  
**Status: Supported**  
Jaggery is 60–85% sucrose by composition (varying by variety and processing). Both jaggery and white sugar deliver sucrose, which is hydrolyzed to glucose and fructose in identical fashion regardless of source. The biochemical claim is correct. Jaggery does contain trace minerals (iron, potassium, magnesium) and antioxidants that white sugar lacks, but the quantities are nutritionally insignificant at typical serving sizes and do not alter the glycemic impact. A physician would appropriately advise diabetics to restrict jaggery and honey as they would any sucrose/fructose source.

**Claim 3: "Refrigerating food doesn't cause it to lose significant nutrients — only water-soluble vitamins B and C are affected, and you can add those back easily."**  
**Status: Supported**  
Refrigeration slows oxidation and microbial degradation. Macronutrients (protein, carbs, fat) are completely stable in the refrigerator. Vitamin C is the most vulnerable water-soluble vitamin and does degrade over time even under refrigeration (though much more slowly than at room temperature). B vitamins have variable stability, with B1 (thiamine) being the most sensitive. The creator's remedy — add a side salad or squeeze of lime — is practical and correct for replenishing vitamin C. The "loss of prana" framing has no scientific basis.

**Claim 4: "Relative vs. absolute risk misuse: 0.1% → 0.2% cancer risk = '100% increase' headline — but the absolute risk is still tiny."**  
**Status: Correct and important**  
This is one of the most important concepts in risk communication. Relative risk inflation is a well-documented phenomenon in health journalism and is explicitly critiqued in EBM methodology. A 100% relative risk increase from 0.1% to 0.2% represents only a 0.1 percentage point absolute increase in risk — typically not clinically meaningful. The creator is accurately describing how misleading headlines are generated. This is textbook science communication.

**Claim 5 (implied): Most "carcinogenic" scares in food content involve doses that are far removed from realistic dietary exposure.**  
**Status: Supported**  
Toxicological principle: "the dose makes the poison." The IARC carcinogen classification system is frequently misunderstood — Group 2A/2B classifications mean there is some evidence of hazard at some dose, not that normal dietary exposure causes cancer. This is a well-documented communication problem in food science.

- **Spiegelhalter D.** "The Art of Statistics: How to Learn from Data." 2019 — Excellent reference on relative vs. absolute risk communication.
- **IARC Monographs:** Classification system frequently misinterpreted; Group 2A/2B ≠ "eating this causes cancer at normal doses."
- **Jaggery composition:** Nutritional databases and peer-reviewed food composition studies confirm 60–85% sucrose content.

## Verdict

All substantive claims in this video are scientifically accurate. Microwaves don't cause cancer (non-ionizing radiation), jaggery is biochemically equivalent to white sugar for metabolic purposes, refrigeration preserves nutrients effectively, and the relative/absolute risk distinction is a critical and underemphasized point in health communication. This is exactly the kind of media literacy content about food science that is needed to counter the wave of pseudoscientific influencer content in India. Highly reliable and useful for building scientific thinking skills.
