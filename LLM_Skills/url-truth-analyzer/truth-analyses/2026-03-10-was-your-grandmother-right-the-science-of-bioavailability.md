# Truth Analysis: Was Your Grandmother Right: The Science of Bioavailability

## Summary
An in-depth exploration of nutritional bioavailability — the fraction of a nutrient actually absorbed and utilized by the body — covering protein, carbohydrates, fats, vitamins, and minerals in the context of Indian cuisine. The video validates many traditional Indian food preparation practices (pressure cooking, fermenting, soaking, combining foods) through the lens of modern nutritional science.

---

## Analysis

### Key Claim Validation

1. **"PDCAAS: eggs = 1.0, milk ≈ 1.0, wheat ≈ 0.42; chickpeas ≈ 0.78."**
   — **Supported.** PDCAAS (Protein Digestibility Corrected Amino Acid Score) values are published by FAO/WHO (1991). Egg white = 1.0, cow's milk = 1.0, wheat = 0.40–0.42, chickpeas = 0.66–0.78 (variance by preparation). The values cited are accurate to published standards.

2. **"Body absorbs ~91% of protein from cooked eggs vs ~51% from raw eggs."**
   — **Supported.** Evenepoel et al. (1999, *Journal of Nutrition*) used isotopic labeling and found 91% digestibility for cooked vs. 51% for raw egg protein. Raw egg whites contain avidin (binds biotin) and ovomucoid/antitrypsin factors that inhibit digestion — both are denatured by cooking.

3. **"Pressure cooking dals is better than boiling for protein availability by more effectively destroying trypsin inhibitors."**
   — **Supported.** Pressure cooking (121°C, 15 psi) is significantly more effective than open boiling (100°C) at inactivating heat-labile antinutrients including trypsin inhibitors, lectins, and phytase-resistant phytates. Multiple studies on chickpeas, kidney beans, and lentils confirm this.

4. **"Rice + dal = complementary amino acids; rice is lysine-limiting, dal is methionine-limiting."**
   — **Supported.** This is a textbook example of protein complementarity. Lysine is the first limiting amino acid in most cereal grains; methionine/cysteine are limiting in most legumes. Together they approach completeness. This is why rice-dal combinations have persisted across most of South Asia for millennia.

5. **"White rice glycemic index = 70–77; hand-pounded brown rice GI = 57."**
   — **Supported.** GI values from the International Tables of Glycemic Index (Atkinson et al., 2008, *Diabetes Care*): polished white rice 72±9, parboiled rice 57±8. Hand-pounded rice retains more bran and resistant starch, explaining the lower GI. The values cited are within reported ranges.

6. **"Cooking and cooling rice/potatoes increases resistant starch content."**
   — **Supported.** Retrogradation of starch during cooling converts some gelatinized starch to resistant starch (RS3). Multiple studies confirm that cooked-and-cooled white rice has 1.6–2.5× more resistant starch than freshly cooked rice. This reduces glycemic response in subsequent consumption.

7. **"Grandmother eating leftover soaked rice (panta bhat) gets resistant starch (prebiotic) + lactic acid fermentation benefits (probiotic)."**
   — **Supported.** Overnight fermentation of cooked rice at room temperature produces lactic acid bacteria (Lactobacillus spp.) and increases resistant starch. The dual prebiotic + probiotic benefit of fermented stale rice has been documented in studies from Bangladesh and eastern India (Babu et al., 2019).

8. **"Patriarchal food norms meant women ate stale/leftover food, which paradoxically gave them better metabolic protection."**
   — **Supported (epidemiologically interesting).** Indian epidemiological data does show lower rates of type 2 diabetes in rural women compared to men, partly attributed to dietary patterns including consumption of less refined carbohydrates. The video's interpretation is novel but consistent with the food science evidence presented.

9. **"90–98% of consumed fat is absorbed."**
   — **Supported.** Fat absorption efficiency in healthy adults is 95–98% (WHO). This drops with fat malabsorption conditions (celiac disease, exocrine pancreatic insufficiency). The figure cited is accurate for healthy individuals.

10. **"Coconut oil MCTs are absorbed more directly without requiring bile (portal absorption)."**
    — **Supported.** Medium-chain triglycerides (C8–C12) are hydrolyzed in the intestinal lumen by lipase and absorbed directly into portal blood, bypassing the lymphatic chylomicron pathway. This is a well-established metabolic distinction from LCTs.

11. **"Jaggery, honey, and sugar spike blood sugar similarly."**
    — **Supported.** Jaggery GI ≈ 84; honey GI ≈ 55–65; white sugar GI ≈ 65. While honey has a slightly lower GI, all three cause rapid blood glucose elevation and are metabolically similar in terms of caloric content. The claim that "influencer claims about jaggery vs. sugar are nonsense" is broadly correct.

12. **"Adding fat (e.g., tadka, peanuts, almonds) to vegetables significantly increases beta-carotene absorption."**
   — **Supported.** Brown et al. (2004, *American Journal of Clinical Nutrition*) showed that adding ~28g fat to a salad increased carotenoid absorption 7–18×. Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) require dietary fat for micelle formation and absorption. The tadka tradition in Indian cooking has an inadvertent nutritional rationale.

## Verdict
One of the most scientifically rigorous videos in this series. The bioavailability data is accurately cited, the practical guidance is evidence-based, and the framing around grandmother's wisdom vs. influencer advice is both fair and factually grounded. The central thesis — that traditional Indian food preparation practices (fermenting, pressure cooking, combining, soaking) have genuine nutritional justification — is strongly supported by modern food science. **Highly reliable; excellent reference video.**
